5. Designing Effective Prompts for Grading

Prompt quality matters because grading tasks are rarely generic. Weak prompts invite vague academic language, generic praise, or shallow summaries of student work. Strong prompts define the evaluative task narrowly enough that the output serves a specific grading purpose.

Useful grading prompts usually supply context, criteria, and a bounded task. They tell the system what kind of intellectual work matters, what role it is playing, and what the output should look like.

The more bounded the task, the more usable the output.

Prompt Contrast

“Give feedback on this paper.”

“Using this rubric and these instructor notes, draft feedback that explains the paper’s main strength, main weakness, and one specific revision priority. Focus on argument quality rather than grammar.”

Prompt Design by Task

For calibration
  • provide the rubric
  • provide one or more anchor examples
  • ask whether the current evaluation is consistent with those standards
For thinking-level diagnosis
  • define the mode of thinking expected
  • ask what mode is actually present
  • require evidence for the classification
For feedback drafting
  • provide brief instructor notes
  • specify the comment structure
  • state what the feedback should prioritize
For post-grading pattern review
  • provide anonymized excerpts or grading notes
  • ask for recurring patterns rather than individual judgments
  • require a concise instructional summary

6. What Must Remain Human

Faculty remain responsible for the meaning of quality in the discipline and for the consequences attached to a grade. AI can support parts of the process, but it does not remove responsibility for judgment, interpretation, or accountability.

This matters most where evaluation depends on nuance, context, or disciplinary judgment that cannot be reduced to a pattern match.

The final evaluation remains the instructor’s responsibility, even when parts of the workflow are AI-assisted.

Faculty Still Decide

  • what counts as strong work in the field
  • which weaknesses are central rather than incidental
  • how to weigh originality, ambiguity, and uneven performance
  • how to interpret context not fully visible in the submitted text
